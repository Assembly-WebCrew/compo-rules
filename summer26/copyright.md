# On Copyright

Every year, a handful of entries run into copyright trouble. Almost always the
author had no bad intent: they used a sample they liked, drew a character they
grew up with, or found an image online with no notice on it. The result is the
same either way, so this page explains how copyright works at Assembly, why we
are strict about it, and what to do when you are unsure.

If your case is not answered here, **ask us before the deadline** at
compo-contact@assembly.org or on the #demoscene channel of the Assembly Discord.
Asking costs you nothing. Finding out on the compo floor costs you the entry.

## Why we are strict

Three things make this less forgiving than a demo party where you just show
something to your friends.

**There is money involved.** Assembly pays real prize money. The moment an entry
that infringes someone's rights wins a cash prize, it stops being a hobbyist
question and becomes a commercial one, both for you and for us. A rights holder
who might shrug at a free scene production will not shrug at a paid competition.

**Entries do not disappear after the party.** We record them, stream them,
publish them on YouTube and archive them. An entry shown once on Friday can
still be online in ten years. A logo or a sample that nobody noticed on the
compo floor is a permanent, searchable, and easily reported problem.

**Finnish law applies, and it has no "fair use".** Assembly is held in Finland,
so Finnish copyright law governs every entry. There is no fair use doctrine
here. Finnish law does have a narrow right of quotation, but it is far narrower
than fair use and does not let you use someone else's work as raw material for
your own. Do not build an entry on the assumption that a US-style exception will
save it.

Because of all this, the burden of proof is on you. If we have to ask about a
piece of material, you need to be able to show where it came from and what
allows you to use it. An entry we cannot clear is not shown.

## The basics

Copyright protects a work automatically, from the moment it is created. There is
no registration, no notice requirement, and no form to fill in. This means:

- **No copyright notice does not mean free.** An image found on a website with
  no © symbol is still protected.
- **Being publicly available does not mean free.** Something being on YouTube,
  Spotify, an image search, or a games archive says nothing about your right to
  use it.
- **Length does not matter.** Five seconds of a song is still part of the song.
  There is no minimum below which copying is safe.
- **Non-commercial intent does not matter here.** Even if you personally make no
  money, this is a competition with cash prizes at a commercial event.

## Using other people's material

**Can I use a few seconds of a released song in my demo?**
No. Short samples of a copyrighted recording are still covered.

**I made a cover version. That is different, right?**
No. A cover is a new performance of someone else's composition and needs the
rights holder's permission. We do not allow covers in any competition.

**What about a remix, or a rearrangement in a different style?**
Same answer. A remix and an arrangement are both derived from the original work
and need permission from the rights holder of that original. Rearranging a song
for a chip platform does not make it your own composition. This exact case has
come up: an author argued that a re-arrangement was neither a cover nor a remix,
and the entry still could not be accepted.

**I asked the composer personally and they said yes.**
That is enough only if they still hold the rights themselves. If they are a
member of a collecting society such as Teosto or Gramex, or an equivalent
society in another country, they have transferred the administration of those
rights to the society and can no longer grant you that permission on their own.

**I bought a sample library. Can I use those sounds?**
Yes, that is what sample libraries are licensed for, as long as you follow the
licence. This is completely different from sampling a released song or film,
which is not allowed. The rule of thumb: a library sold to be used in music is
fine, a recording sold to be listened to is not.

**Can I use footage or audio from a film, a game, or a TV show?**
No, not without a licence covering it.

## Creative Commons, public domain and stock material

**Is Creative Commons or public domain material allowed?**
Yes, where the competition's own rules allow it, on two conditions: you follow
the licence, and you **declare it in the entry form with a link to the source**.
Licensed material that you do not declare is treated exactly like unlicensed
material.

Following the licence means all of it. Most CC licences require attribution,
some require share-alike, and NonCommercial (NC) licences prohibit commercial
use. Assembly pays prize money and is a commercial event, so **do not assume
NC-licensed material is safe here**. Ask first.

**Does using CC or public domain material break the "previously unreleased"
rule?**
No, and this is worth stating clearly because the rules are easy to misread.
The "previously unreleased" requirement is about **your own work**: the entry as
a whole, and the parts of it that are its main creative effort, must not have
been released before. Third-party components used under a valid licence, such as
a font, an instrument sample library, or a public domain photograph, do not make
your entry "previously released", as long as they are not the main effort of the
entry.

**What counts as "the main effort"?**
The parts a viewer would say the entry is made of. A licensed font in the
credits is not the main effort. A licensed music track that a rhythm game is
built around is. If you are not sure which side of the line you are on, ask us
before the deadline. The preselection jury makes the final call.

**Stock photos?**
Allowed in competitions whose rules permit them, if your licence covers this
use, and declared in the entry form. The clear majority of the entry must still
be your own creative work. Note that some competitions ban them outright, for
example Pixel / Oldskool Graphics does not allow stock photos or paintovers at
all. Check the compo's own rules.

## Trademarks and logos

Trademarks are protected separately from copyright, and the protection is about
identifying a company's products rather than about creativity. That makes them a
different kind of risk: even a small, lovingly pixelled logo is a use of someone
else's mark.

**Can I show a console maker's logo as a retro homage?**
No. This has been asked more than once, most recently about a hardware
manufacturer's mark redrawn in an entry. The answer has been the same each time:
a registered trademark needs permission, and because we archive and re-show
entries afterwards, a detail that seems harmless during the compo stays online
indefinitely.

**So how do I do retro nostalgia then?**
Redraw it as something recognisably your own. Evoke the era, the hardware, the
palette and the feel: none of that belongs to anyone. Just do not reproduce the
mark itself.

## Fan art

**Is fan art allowed?**
No. An entry whose subject is a character, a world, or a work owned by someone
else is not accepted, however skilfully it is made. Finnish law has no fan art
exception, and with cash prizes on the table we do not take the risk.

**Where exactly is the line between fan art and inspiration?**
Style, genre, technique and era are free. Nobody owns a look. What is not free is
a **specific protected work or character**.

A practical test: if an average viewer would name the source when looking at
your entry, it is fan art. If they would only name a style or a mood, it is
inspiration.

- A painting in the style of a well-known fantasy illustrator, with your own
  subject: **inspiration**, allowed.
- A painting of a named character from a film, a game or a comic: **fan art**,
  not allowed.
- A demo whose look is built on the aesthetics of a 1980s arcade era: fine.
- A demo built around a recognisable arcade character: not fine.

Borderline cases exist and we would rather look at them in advance than
disqualify you afterwards. Send us a description or a work-in-progress shot and
we will answer in writing.

**Parody?**
Finnish law gives parody much less room than some other jurisdictions. Do not
rely on it without asking us first.

## AI-generated content

**Which competitions allow AI-generated content?**
Only those whose rules say so explicitly. AI Coding (Vibe Demo) does. In every
other competition, the entry's own content, the code, graphics and music, must be
human-made. Tools that were themselves built with AI, such as synths, trackers,
packers, compilers and editors, are fine to use.

**My AI service's paid plan grants me full commercial rights to the output.
Does that make it allowed?**
Those are two separate questions. Rights from the provider may settle whether
you are allowed to **use** the output at all, but they do not make AI content
allowed in a competition whose rules ban it. And in competitions where AI
content is allowed, you still need those rights and you must declare the tools
you used.

## If something is wrong

An entry that we cannot clear is disqualified and not shown. If a problem is
found after the competition, the result can be revised and prize money withheld.
You remain responsible for any infringement your entry causes.

This sounds harsh, and it is meant to be predictable rather than harsh: we would
much rather answer a question in June than remove an entry on Saturday night.

## How to document your rights

Entries with clean documentation almost never become a problem. In the entry
form, and in a readme inside your submission, list:

- what the third-party material is,
- where it came from, with a link,
- and under what licence or permission you are using it.

For a personal permission, keep the message where it was given. For a purchased
licence, keep the receipt or licence page.

## Finnish rights organisations

If you need to clear rights or check who administers them:

- **Teosto** - composers, lyricists and publishers (musical works)
- **Gramex** - performing artists and record producers (recordings)
- **Kopiosto** - publications
- **Kuvasto** - visual arts
- **IFPI Finland** - recordings

## Still unsure?

Ask. compo-contact@assembly.org, or the #demoscene channel on the Assembly
Discord. There is no such thing as a stupid copyright question, and every year
the people who ask are the ones whose entries make it to the screen.
